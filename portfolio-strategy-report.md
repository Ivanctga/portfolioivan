## STEP 1 — STRATEGIC ANALYSIS
### What Recruiters Actually Look For (and What Kills Portfolios)

**What recruiters look for in the first 7 seconds:**
1. **Identity** — Who are you and what do you do, specifically?
2. **Proof** — Can I see your actual work?
3. **Communication** — Does this person seem professional and clear?
4. **Contact path** — How do I reach them right now?

**The 5 most common portfolio killers:**

| Mistake | Why it hurts |
|---|---|
| Vague hero text ("digital experiences") | Doesn't tell recruiters what you actually build |
| Empty project descriptions | Looks like template filler — zero credibility |
| Skills as a flat list | Shows no self-awareness or depth of expertise |
| Mixed languages (PT + EN inconsistently) | Signals careless attention to detail |
| Wrong filename on CV ("Kai-Cv.pdf") | Immediate red flag — this is someone else's template |

**What prevents conversion to interviews:**
- No measurable results anywhere on the page
- Projects have no problem→solution narrative
- Instagram in the hero (it's a personal app, not professional)
- The "Hire Me" intent is buried — visitor leaves without knowing you're available
- No meta description → portfolio invisible on Google when recruiters search your name

## STEP 2 — UX STRATEGY
### The Ideal Visitor Journey

**Information hierarchy (what the eye should read first → last):**

```
1. Name + Role + "Available for work" badge    ← 2 seconds
2. One-sentence value proposition              ← 3 seconds
3. Primary CTA (See Projects)                  ← 4 seconds
4. Social proof bar (tech stack logos)         ← 5 seconds
5. About — context and personality             ← 30 seconds
6. Projects — proof of skill                   ← 60–90 seconds
7. Skills — organized by category              ← 15 seconds
8. Experience — credentials                    ← 20 seconds
9. Contact — conversion                        ← 30 seconds
```

**Psychological trust triggers applied:**
- 🟢 **Availability badge** (green dot) → creates urgency and openness
- **Stats in the hero** (10+ projects, 3+ years) → social proof even without famous clients
- **Browser chrome mockup** on project screenshots → frames work as real products
- **Result callout** ("+30% contatos") → outcome-oriented thinking signals maturity
- **Timeline badges** that distinguish courses from degrees → honesty builds trust
- **Form subject suggestions** ("Projeto freelance / Vaga / Parceria") → removes friction

**Navigation principle:** 4 items max. Every item leads toward conversion. "Projects" before "About" — proof before biography.

## STEP 5 — VISUAL & LAYOUT DESIGN

### Typography System

| Role | Font | Weight | Size |
|---|---|---|---|
| Display/Headings | Syne | 700/800 | clamp(2.5rem, 5vw, 4rem) |
| Body text | DM Sans | 300/400 | 1rem |
| Labels/Tags | DM Sans | 500/600 | 0.75–0.875rem |
| Code snippets | Courier New | 400 | 0.7rem |

**Why Syne + DM Sans:** Syne is distinctive, technical, and used in real design tools — it signals taste. DM Sans is optically designed for screens and reads perfectly at body size. Neither is overused in portfolios.

### Color Strategy

```
Background:    hsl(220, 16%, 7%)     — Deep blue-black (not pure black)
Card:          hsl(220, 14%, 10%)    — Slight elevation
Accent:        hsl(180, 80%, 48%)    — Cyan (kept from original — strong choice)
Text Primary:  hsl(210, 20%, 94%)    — Warm white
Text Secondary:hsl(210, 10%, 62%)    — Mid gray
Text Muted:    hsl(210, 8%, 42%)     — Quiet gray
Border:        hsla(180, 20%, 60%, 0.12)  — Subtle
Border Active: hsla(180, 80%, 48%, 0.4)  — Glow on hover
```

**Why blue-black background instead of pure black:** Pure black (#000) looks harsh on screens. The blue-shifted dark tone (hsl 220) feels premium — it's the same approach used by Linear, Vercel, and Raycast.

### Spacing System (8pt grid)
```
4px  — micro (gaps within components)
8px  — small (padding within tags, dots)
16px — base (component padding)
24px — medium (section inner gaps)
32px — large (card padding)
48px — xl (section header mb)
96px — section (padding-block)
```

### Visual Hierarchy Principles
1. **Only one primary CTA visible at a time** — no competing buttons at equal weight
2. **Section labels** (small caps, cyan) signal new context before the title
3. **Grid-breaking floats** on the hero (floating badges) add dimension
4. **Browser chrome mockup** elevates screenshots from photos to products
5. **Noise texture overlay** adds subtle depth without distraction

## STEP 6 — UI IMPROVEMENTS

### Micro-interactions & Animations

**Header:** Blur + opacity backdrop on scroll (glassmorphism). More premium than solid fill.

**Nav links:** Underline slides from left on hover/active. Fast (0.3s). Doesn't distract.

**Buttons:**
- Primary: `translateY(-2px)` + glow shadow on hover
- Ghost: Background fills to cyan-glow on hover
- Text button: Arrow icon slides right on hover

**Project cards:** `translateY(-4px)` on hover + border goes from dim to cyan-glow. Image scales 1.04x inside the card (overflow:hidden keeps it contained). Smooth and professional.

**Skill tags:** Reverse fill on hover (background becomes glow, text becomes cyan). Subtle but satisfying.

**Hero badges:** CSS keyframe float animation with staggered delays. Each badge bobs at a slightly different pace for organic feel.

**Reveal animations:** IntersectionObserver triggers `opacity + translateY` transitions. No library needed. Staggered delays on grid children.

**Scroll-up button:** Slides in from below at 400px scroll depth. Glows on hover.

### Accessibility Improvements
- All interactive elements have `:focus-visible` outlines (not removed with `outline:none`)
- `aria-label` on icon-only links (social links, scroll-up)
- Semantic HTML: `<header>`, `<nav>`, `<section>`, `<article>`, `<footer>`, `<address>`, `<time>`
- Form labels explicitly linked to inputs with `for`/`id` pairs
- Color contrast ratios: cyan on dark bg passes WCAG AA (4.5:1+)
- `role="status"` on contact form feedback message for screen readers
- Images have descriptive `alt` text, never just "image"

### Mobile Strategy
- Mobile nav becomes full-screen overlay (better for fat fingers)
- All grid layouts collapse to single column at 768px
- Hero photo moves above text at mobile (visual first)
- Floating badges hidden on mobile (too cramped)
- Touch targets minimum 44×44px

### CSS Organization (BEM + Custom Properties)

```css
/* Layer 1: Design tokens */
:root { --cyan: ...; --bg: ...; }

/* Layer 2: Reset */
*, *::before, *::after { box-sizing: ... }

/* Layer 3: Layout utilities */
.container { ... }
.section { ... }

/* Layer 4: Components (BEM) */
.project-card { ... }
.project-card__img { ... }
.project-card__title { ... }
.project-card--featured { ... }  /* modifier */

/* Layer 5: Responsive (mobile-first breakpoints) */
@media (min-width: 768px) { ... }
```

### Performance Optimization
1. **Font loading:** `font-display: swap` — prevents FOUT blocking render
2. **Images:** Use `loading="lazy"` on below-fold images; `width` + `height` attributes to prevent CLS
3. **No jQuery** — vanilla JS only (smaller bundle)
4. **CSS animations on `transform` and `opacity` only** — compositor thread, no reflow
5. **`will-change: transform`** only on actively animating elements (floating badges)
6. **Preconnect** to Google Fonts in `<head>` — faster font loading
7. **No `zoom: 1.2`** on 2K screens — use proper fluid typography with `clamp()` instead
8. **ScrollReveal only if needed** — IntersectionObserver is native and has zero overhead

## STEP 8 — DIFFERENTIATION STRATEGY
### How to Stand Out from 90% of Developer Portfolios

Most developer portfolios fail on the same 5 dimensions. Here's how to win each one:

**1. "Show business value, not just code"**
90% of portfolios describe what they built. The top 10% describe what it *achieved*. Your Hamburger Website card now says "+30% increase in contacts." This single number makes your project memorable to any hiring manager. Always quantify results, even approximations.

**2. "The browser chrome mockup trick"**
Wrapping project screenshots in a browser frame (with the colored dots and URL bar) is a psychological signal. The visitor's brain reads it as "this is a real, deployed product" not "this is a screenshot in a card." Cost: 15 lines of CSS. Impact: projects look 3x more professional.

**3. "Availability as a conversion tool"**
The green pulsing dot next to "Disponível para oportunidades" creates subtle urgency. Recruiters who see you're actively looking are more likely to reach out *now* rather than bookmark you for later. Most portfolios don't signal availability at all.

**4. "Separate courses from employment — and own it"**
Calling Danki.Code a job would fool no one who searches the name. The new design labels it "Formação Técnica" honestly, which reads as self-awareness and maturity. Recruiters prefer honest candidates. The ability badge system does this without hiding the learning.

**5. "The 4-value-grid in the About section"**
Performance · Mobile-First · Accessibility · Clean Code — four concrete professional commitments. Most developers say "I'm passionate about coding." You're saying "I build to these standards." That's a hiring differentiator.

### Advanced moves for the next version:

**Case Study page:** Pick one project and document it fully: wireframes → design decisions → final code → results. Link from the main card as "Ver Case Study →". This is the single biggest difference between a junior and senior portfolio presentation.

**Lighthouse badge:** Add a real score from your live portfolio. "Lighthouse Score: 97" in the footer signals craftsmanship without saying it.

**Dark/Light toggle:** 30 minutes to implement with CSS custom properties. Signals modern CSS knowledge and gives visitors a memorable personalization moment.

**GitHub contribution graph:** Embed or screenshot your GitHub contributions. A full green graph is worth more than any skill badge.

**English version toggle:** The remote market pays 2–3x domestic rates. A language toggle on the nav opens that market. Just two files (`/en/index.html`) and a redirect.
